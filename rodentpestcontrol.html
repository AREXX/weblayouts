What an infestation actually sounds like

Most calls start the same way — a noise at night, a smell that's hard to place, a pet that won't stop staring at the wall. Here's what our inspectors look and listen for first.

Scratching or scurrying in the attic, worst after dark

Gnaw marks on wiring, wood trim, or stored boxes

Dark, greasy rub marks along baseboards and corners

Shredded insulation pulled into loose piles or tunnels

A pet suddenly fixated on one wall, vent, or cabinet

A sharp, ammonia-like smell in an enclosed space

Anatomy of an entry point

A mouse fits through a gap the width of a pencil. A rat only needs about a quarter. Most homes have more of these gaps than the owner realizes — here's where we check first.

01

Roofline & soffit gaps

Where the roof deck meets the fascia — the single most common way rats get into a Texas attic.

02

Attic & gable vents

Unscreened vents look like ventilation to a homeowner and an open door to a rodent.

03

Plumbing & utility penetrations

Pipe and cable entry points are rarely sealed tight after the original install.

04

Garage door corners

Worn weatherstripping leaves a corner gap that's easy to miss from eye level.

05

Foundation cracks & weep holes

Common in shifting Gulf Coast soil, and often overlooked during a visual walkthrough.

What we clear

Mostly rats and mice. Occasionally, something bigger.

The core of our work is Norway rats, roof rats, and house mice — the species actually classified as rodents, and the ones behind almost every attic call we get. Every so often an opossum or raccoon finds the same gap and moves in first. We handle those too, humanely, then seal the entry point so nothing takes its place.

Tap a species for details & a photo →

Norway rat

The larger of the two rats we see in Katy — heavy-bodied, blunt nose, small close-set ears. They burrow at ground level, favoring foundations, crawlspaces, and gaps near the slab rather than climbing into attics.

Roof rat

Smaller and sleeker than a Norway rat, with a pointed nose, large ears, and a tail longer than its body. Excellent climbers — this is the one we find in attics, soffits, and upper wall cavities.

House mouse

Small, gray-brown, and the single most common call we get. A mouse can squeeze through a gap the width of a pencil, and one pair can produce dozens of offspring in a year if left alone.

Opossum (occasional)

North America's only marsupial, not a rodent at all. Docile and nocturnal — if a gap is big enough, one will den under a house or in an attic, usually with a litter in tow.

Not a rodent — but still not paying rent.

Raccoon (occasional)

Dexterous enough to pry open loose soffit and roof vents, and strong enough to widen the gap once inside. More destructive than rodents, and handled the same way — humanely, with the entry point sealed after.
